Final Considerations

Now that you've seen a few specific cases - all of them intentionally flawed in some way - let's go over some general guidelines.

  1. Build only what you need. Many courses do not need alternative navigation. If your learners will be better served by moving through the course in order, let them do that and spend your effort elsewhere.
  2. Test, test, test. Make sure that a lot of people see your navigation before it gets into the world. Get feedback from all of them.
  3. Have a goal. Your navigation should help your learners do something specific. Do you want them to be able to skip past things they already know? Should they get a better understanding of the interconnection between topics? Will your navigation set the context for what your learners see? Your navigation will be most effecive if you pick a single purpose and build toward it.
  4. Have a backup plan. Your navigation might break some day - or you might simply make a typo that leaves a page inaccessible! If you can bear to leave edX's existing navigation in place, do so. If you can do without fancy javascript libraries, do without.
  5. Follow design principles. As the saying goes, "The opposite of good design is not no design, it's bad design."
